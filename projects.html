WebGL

My background is in computer graphics. It's my first love, and I can trace that all the way back to when I first started programming little games on the Atari 400 computer my Dad would bring home from work on the weekends (pre-1980). I periodically like to revisit the topic to keep up with modern trends.

OpenGL hasn't been my native tongue since leaving SGI in the late 1990s, but it's become the standard for 3D on the web. I looked at a few of the well-known packages, like three.js, and decided I was more interested in learning the lower-level API and developing my own "middleware" with a bias towards the types of things I want to draw.

Thus, WebGL.js was born, a very simple rendering interface. I expect that I will keep coming back to this over many years.

Astro

While working in the aerospace industry on Space Situational Awareness (SSA) topics, I realized I needed a better understanding of concepts that were everyday to the aerospace engineers. Being a visual guy, I figured I'd make it a visual exploration. Astro.js is the manifestation of that, built using the aforementioned WebGL.js library.

The model includes WGS-84 planetary geometry with "real" day and night textures assembled from NASA's Blue Marble project, cloud textures based on GEOS satellite infrared imagery, and physically based approximations of the atmospheric affect on the appearance of the Earth to an outside observer. I added a variety of cameras to the scene to look at the Earth-Moon-Sol system in a variety of new ways.

I found it rather challenging to validate my results, so I turned to other sources, including satellite and other imagery from various space missions to compare my images with "real" results. I learned a lot, and want to continue exploring this domain.

Another challenge I encountered was the size of the starfield texture I was using. In order to be good quality, I was using a PNG file that was over 40Mb. I could have used one of the typical texture compression formats to try to get that smaller, but instead I hunted down the Yale Bright Star Catalog and converted it to JSON. I used the star temperature information, or the classification if temperature wasn't available, to set the color. This file is MUCH smaller, and I generate geometry for the stars rendered at screen resolution. Note that it does not include Nebula or other Messier objects, of which a few are significant members of prominent constellations.

I became fascinated with some of the eclipse imagery from the EPIC/DISCOVR mission. Shadows are a challenge in Z-buffered rendering (like OpenGL), but in this case with only one occluder, I thought it would be pretty straight-forward. I had some ideas about the math for computing umbra and penumbra analytically, which I explored in a circle/circle intersection visualization.

Using the circle intersection visualization, I determined a much simpler approximation that was a pretty good fit, which is what I use in the Earth shader. I used the same computations on the Moon shader, so you can explore both solar and lunar eclipses.

Epidimiology

I fancy myself an amateur epidimiologist. I studied the topic enough to be able to talk about disease spread intelligently, because I found myself wondering about potential contagions like Ebola, while close friends were dealing with issues around HPV and cervical cancer. One of my conclusions is that the population-level study of disease has been a great boon to goverment handling of potential epidemics, but it completely forgets the human element of health.

My thesis is that by empowering individuals to make smarter choices, we can reduce or eliminate the spread of certain pathogens that are otherwise considered "contained" by the population-level study. For many of people who suffer from these diseases, they are life changing.

My Disease Transmission Simulator was born out of a desire to visualize the details of transmission at an individual level using stochastic techniques to approximate the behavior of small groups as proxies for individuals. This simulation is validated by the fact that it exhibits many of the classic population-level phenomena.

The simulation is built in pure Javascript using charting tools and tree-visualization tools available on GitHub (which I also wrote).
